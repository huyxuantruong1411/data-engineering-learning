          </div><div class="tags"><div class="tag recommended btn-label js-btn-label" data-id="1" data-href="https://myanimelist.net/manga/137939/Chi_Chikyuu_no_Undou_ni_Tsuite/reviews"><i class="fas fa-star"></i>Recommended</div></div>      <div class="text">
                Orb. On The Movement of The Earth is a very enjoyable manga and a great experience. The story itself is not a jaw dropping masterpiece, it’s just okay, but the themes explored during these 62 chapters make up for it tenfolds. <br />
I love the way their mangaka was able to depict the time period and area in which the story was set and the way people from multiple parts of the society at that time and place are represented with their hardships and challenges. <br />
The characters personalities themselves were for me hard to cling on to, but the way their ideals and philosophies were portrayed
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">was amazing. The philosophical ideas in this manga is what elevates it from being just another “mid” story. <br />
I think the best character this is seen with is the stories antagonist. Throughout his arc we get to know him as a person and his motivations and we start to be able to sympathise with him even though we’re still “against” him so to speak. I ended up hoping there was a way where he also could get his own happy ending.<br />
<br />
There are a few double page spreads that are stunning in here, but the art as a whole is just okay, nothing to write home about. <br />
<br />
If only for the philospies and the setting of this story, it would have gotten a 9/10 or even a 10/10 in my book. But the story itself being just subpar brings it down a bit for me, ending up with a 7. <br />
It’s still a great reading experience. If you’re only interested in plot and characters, this might not be for you, but if you like philosophical stories, I recommend at least giving Orb a chance!</span>

                And so, after almost two years, a chapter of my life comes to an end. This story - indeed, this odyssey - comes to a close on this Thursday, February 13, 2025. I don&#039;t know where to begin. This manga has accompanied me through so many stages of my student life that putting these few words down on paper is tearing me apart. Yet, reluctantly, I must move on, turn the page.<br />
<br />
But how can I decently do so? How can I forget this story that has given me so much? A work of unparalleled richness, whether in its masterful development, its characters of rare depth,
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">or the fascinating duality of the last four chapters.<br />

Speaking of which... Given the mixed reviews, I went into this denouement with a certain amount of trepidation. What a terrible mistake! Admittedly, there are still a few grey areas as to the fate of certain protagonists (how did Badeni and Rafal survive?), but beyond these mysteries, Uoto-sensei infuses these final pages with a dazzling presence and wisdom, staging powerful oppositions that shake up our certainties about the figures involved.<br />
<br />
Thus, Douraka and Novak close the loop initiated by Rafal and Novak himself. Albert and Ockzy weave a link between the unfulfilled dream of one and the burning resentment of the other towards the university. But it&#039;s the relationship between Badeni and Rafal that proves the most captivating. Rafal, a curious, dreamy child, aspired to discover and pass on the beauty of the world. Yet, to our great sadness, he lost his way, becoming a cruel being ready to sacrifice everything to achieve his goal. Badeni, on the other hand, took the opposite path. Initially selfish and eager to learn, he had no intention of passing on his knowledge to future generations. But with the big-hearted Ockzy, he discovered empathy and the joy of transmission. And although his friend tragically passed away that fateful night, his memory and kindness live on in him.<br />
<br />
I must confess that his last appearance sealed in my mind a thought that had been haunting me for a long time: Badeni is, without a shadow of a doubt, the most striking character in this work. As a Christian, his unshakeable faith in Our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ warms my heart and gives me the certainty that, in the face of the cruellest trials, God remains our absolute refuge. Faith in Him enables us to overcome any pain, provided we open our hearts to His light.<br />
<br />
<br />
And yet... Despite this declaration of love, I can&#039;t help wondering: how can I turn this page? Will I ever be able to return to a seinen of such stature? A work so singular, where every character, every event, every symbol resonates with such accuracy and emotion? I don&#039;t think so. No story will ever mark my soul the way this one did. Too many emotions intertwine within me, and words themselves seem powerless to express them fully.<br />
<br />
But perhaps, by writing these lines, I have soothed my wounded heart, if only a little. So, I can now say it loud and clear: Du Mouvement de la terre is indeed one of the greatest seinen ever! Thank you to each and every one of these characters for the unforgettable moments spent at their side. I&#039;m going to miss you cruelly, but rest assured of one thing: I&#039;ll never forget you...</span>

<br />
Story: 6/10<br />
The manga has three time skips, each following a different character (well, four including the epilogue). First we&#039;re introduced to the first protagonist and I was excited to see his journey. Then we have a time skip and I realised that there would be no protagonist to follow in this manga. In fact, we seem to follow the ideas each character left behind more
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">than the characters themselves.<br />
<br />
The first part felt too short, it was jarring to move past the first protagonist so soon. The second part was the longest and, predictably, the best. We followed one set of characters for longer and they could be more developed.<br />
<br />
The third part was the worst in my opinion, it felt very out of left field due to how, for lack of a better word, shounen it felt compared to the grounded historical feeling of the rest of the manga.<br />
<br />
I feel like the manga tries to tackle the historical prejudice against women in the era of the setting but it was very surface level and preachy. The main female character is pretty much immediately accepted as an intellectual equal and there is no obstacle to overcome, most characters around her is totally cool with her being a scholar. Seems weird to focus on the difficulties of female scholars when we really only see it being an issue for a chapter or two.<br />
<br />
And yet, despite all my complaints, there is something profound here. The manga dives into the philosophy of faith and science, as well as the fact that they may not be so opposed after all. The characters&#039; motivations behind sacrificing so much merely to advance an idea resonates with me. At times I felt like the author was the one speaking not the characters but their ideas are delivered so earnestly I can&#039;t bring myself to hate it.<br />
<br />
Character: 5/10<br />
The characters are used exclusively as a vehicle for their respective world views. The majority of their dialogue reads more like the author lecturing through the characters than something someone would actually say.<br />
<br />
When I realised that we wouldn&#039;t be following any character for very long, I stopped caring all that much for each one and I wasn&#039;t surprised when I found that they were not very developed. They don&#039;t really do anything except research or talk about heliocentrism.<br />
<br />
The main antagonist, Nowak is genuinely scary - his sheer conviction and brutality make a compelling villain. Considering he&#039;s the only character that is present throughout all three parts, this is especially good.<br />
<br />
Art<br />
Every part of the art is beautiful… except the characters. Buildings, nature, objects, backgrounds, all of it is great but the characters look so strange. Their faces have this strange uncanny valley quality that is genuinely distracting at times. The fact that many characters look very similar does not help. The rest of it is very good though.<br />
<br />
Overall: 6/10<br />
The very definition of not for everyone. Orb as a story feels rather bare-bones but it shows such passion for science and the pursuit of truth that I almost can&#039;t fault it.<br />
<br />
Mixed recommendation: avoid if you&#039;re in it for plot or characters but if you want to understand why people pursue truth, then this manga is for you.</span>

                This is a weird little manga. I very much enjoyed my time with it, though. TL;DR, it&#039;s not an amazing story, it&#039;s really not a good art book but it was fantastic as what is almost a philosophical text.<br />
The story focuses on a range of characters who become entranced by the theory of heliocentrism in a geocentric world and are subsequently hunted by the inquisition. They all share their passion for knowledge and truth and struggle against their faith in the face of what they know to be heretical ideas.<br />
Oh, right, also a warning. It&#039;s a very graphic manga very quickly.<br />
 <br />
The major downside of
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">Orb&#039;s story is that it is far too wordy. Some pages are more on the &#039;book&#039; part of &#039;comic book&#039;, and basically all of its narrative is told through the dialogue (save for a few rare moments). It can be somewhat of a fatiguing and slow read at times because of it. On top of that, its writing is also mixed. It feels strange that the characters have such thoroughly formed ideas and articulate them so well, referencing philosophical texts far beyond the historical setting.<br />
Despite that, the dialogue is doing something right, because I loved listening (or, reading) the clashing views of each of the characters. These characters each strive to realise their meaning and ultimately find it through knowledge and freedom to different extents, and oftentimes end up debating and coming to terms with the conflict of their incomplete knowledge, which gives plenty to consider in the course of its length. Though, if the manga wanted a more compelling narrative, I feel there are ways it could&#039;ve been done. I would kill for a story about Jolenta&#039;s life instead of just receiving a timeskip and seeing what came of her. Instead, it follows heliocentrism more than it does the principle characters.<br />
<br />
The art is honestly not very good, and that is the biggest criticism I can possibly draw. It was bad enough at points that it totally ruined the impact of certain scenes. The worst one was during the emotional climax of the story as the main character of the arc looks out over the sunrise, and we get a panel of them looking like an original character from One. The character designs are also very difficult to tell apart. I don&#039;t know why they made one of the last characters we see separate from but identical (including in name) to the first character, but it greatly confused me. There are also some very out there designs, especially with the knights in the latter half of the story which were impossible to take seriously.<br />
<br />
Overall, it would have been worth an 8 if the art was of a more standard quality. For any improvements beyond that, it would have needed to be more deft in its storytelling and careful in its writing. Ultimately, it&#039;s worth giving a go if it sounds like your thing. Give it 10-20 chapters to really get a feel for it</span>

                About the Movement of the Earth is a nice story about humanity&#039;s purpose told through the struggle for heliocentrism. It goes through the lives of its multiple main characters while they fight for this cause, each having their own personal struggles, covering different themes alongside the main one.<br />
<br />
The themes are brought up thoroughly in long dialogue that are tied into the story itself. The characters talk A LOT about philosophical ideas in these blatant lecture-style ramblings (?). It feels more like the characters are having debates in the middle of whatever&#039;s happening in the story. But more often than not, it does feel quite natural.
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">I&#039;m not saying it&#039;s realistic but it does fit the manga&#039;s tone. <br />
<br />
This does get annoying sometimes, and the lack of visual presentation does not help. I don&#039;t mind the &#039;ugly&#039; drawings, but some panels are just bland or even empty. It makes it feel like I&#039;m just reading a script for a play or something. Actually, the story is structured like a stage play. There&#039;s a bunch of characters explicitly ranting towards each other or to themselves, and the scene ends with one big moment. Idk if that&#039;s accurate but the plays I&#039;ve watched are like that (I&#039;ve only watched 2 of them).<br />
<br />
Best part about this manga is its depiction of the value of human life in the pursuit of a greater purpose. There&#039;s something romantic about hundreds and thousands of people giving up their lives for a single cause or idea, especially when this cause ends up lasting an eternity (eg: the pyramids, any religion). The forgotten lives of the workers that built the pyramids might seem meaningless in the grand scheme of things, but the fact that they contributed to one of the greatest legacies of humanity, it gives their lives meaning.<br />
<br />
The reality is that a lot of human greatness was achieved by the sacrifice of many. It&#039;s a theme that I rarely encounter in media. Most of the time the sacrifice of the background characters is not addressed at all in favour of the main ones. About the Movement of the Earth depicts this theme with a bleak honesty on the oppression and suffering experienced by our characters, but also a feel-good aftertaste since heliocentrism did eventually prevail at the end. The fact that every characters struggle ends up being useful, no matter how small it is, also gives the reader some semblance of relief. It probably wasn&#039;t as nice in reality.<br />
<br />
Symbolically, the fight to prove that earth was not at the center of the universe represents the rejection of selfish individual greatness. The sun will keep revolving regardless of what happens to us, same as the lofty ideas that will outlive its famed founders. But the founders themselves are a product of a greater whole. The perceived greatness of Copernicus or any other great man was built on the works of a hundred lives, forgotten by time, just like everything else in the universe.<br />
<br />
Overall a pretty nice manga. 7-8/10<br />

                Spoilers bellow:<br />
<br />
In general, i do not know why this manga has not more feedback. This should have been a widelly read work, becuase it is artistically excelent; intellectually excelent too. I can get one thing that people may not like; this manga does not do favors to the fans; there is no plot armor at all, and at first, i did not appreciate it (once rafael dies, after you start liking him and start feeling you will go to the big journay with him, you get then indroduced to some people you do not know, only for the same proccess to repeat). Anyways lets go
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">piece by piece.<br />
<br />
ART: I personally did not like the art that much. Too simplistic, but you still could make out different characters from their faces and not hair style. If the reader expects to see beautifull art, this wont cut it, most of the art here is drawn as simplistically as possible. The drawings arent the subject here, it is clear the writer invested more on the story and the feelings he wanted to convery to the reader throught dialogue. In this sense, the story and dialogue over-conmpensates for the art. All in all, i would put the art a good 7.<br />
<br />
STORY: This is the strong part of this work. The story. The writer manages to build it up, with you being hooked, and hoping anyone from the original crew would survive, with none surviving. They all die out, one by one, but as Rafael says to Novak at the end of the story &quot;We all have been cooperators in this&quot;. This is the feeling this manga wants to convey; the feeling of belonging to something big. This is a manga about people who mostly fight for their own individual porpuses, ending up nonetheless sacrificing themselves for a greater porpuse, and contributing to the knowledge we have today. The writer is also not a chauvinist in the sense that the intellectuals of this story are only the intellectuals; it starts and ends with intellectuals indeed, but the main protagonists of the story are not at all intellectuals; it is terrorists, mercenaries, defectors from the system and gipsies. The arcs of the intellectuals (basically, rafal and albert) compromise not more than 10 chapters from the 62. It can easelly be said that 90% of the story is the writer telling you that every human being, if so they desire, can be an intellectual in equal footing with the top of society who produce the mainstream intellectuals. The concept of revolution and theology is importand here. What is also importand is that the writer really, really did do so much work and reading and thought before writing this, so much philoshophical and historical dialogue, and for things far from his japanese society too. One can appreciate that the writer was not at all lazy in this work. All in all, the story gets a 10<br />
<br />
CHARACTERS: As it is being said above, if you are here looking to identify with a character throught all the story, you are wrong. There is not a single main character here being an idealized version of the &#039;good guy&#039;; not only this, there is no plot armor for them. They all end up getting what they would get in real life, outside of plot armors. This is becuase the characters are only engines for the meaning of the story the writer wants to tell, and by them passing the way they pass, the feeling that all this, our world, is not the work of &#039;great men&#039; or a single man, but throught the work of generations and generations (remiding our foolish generation that thinks we are &#039;smarter&#039; than the &#039;idiotic&#039; ancestors) that we see high, becuase we are stepping in the shoulders of giants. Nonetheless, there is character development, which makes the story even better; when the characters die, you feel sad. I dare say, all can identify, becuase the characters are just so real, from the first to the last. Rafal was an elitist bastard who wanted to make people work for him and exploit them, yet, while his desire started from his individualism, he growed to embrace the whole, and came to understand himself as part of the bigger picture. Not only this, but rafal ended up dying for his convictions. The reader may think that this was all for nothing, but 10 years later, it seems that two random mercenaries, with one being a little interesting in astrology, and the other not being able to look the beauty stars out of his shame that he is so filthy (he kills people for money after all), and not even being able to read, by luck find out his legacy. The story begins again, and mercenary oczy with the help of monk rebel badeni continiue what rafal left unfinished. Oczy is basically the protagonist of this story; he has the biggest character development, and it is his book that links the past, present and future. Next up, we have a group of revolutionary terrorists lead by Oczy&#039;s former friend, and a captain named Schmitt trying to find Oczy&#039;s lost book where the theory of the earth moving has been written in a simple manner for mass consuption so everyone can understand it, and then next we have draka&#039;s arc, where a gipsy tries to become a big time capitalists ends up abandoning all this for the sake of the &#039;greater whole&#039;. The last thing she does is send a message about the book, which by sheer luck, Albert Brudzewski hears the title off (the only thing that survived from the work), and starts thinkng about this theory seriously. Then, he teaches Copernicus, and we have the copernecian revolution. All in all, 10 here too.<br />
<br />
Overall score, 10.</span>

      <div title="3:54 AM" class="update_at">Jul 29, 2022</div><div class="username">
          <a href="https://myanimelist.net/profile/Zrisha" class="ga-click" data-ga-click-type="review-manga-reviewer">Zrisha</a>
          </div><div class="tags"><div class="tag recommended btn-label js-btn-label" data-id="1" data-href="https://myanimelist.net/manga/137939/Chi_Chikyuu_no_Undou_ni_Tsuite/reviews"><i class="fas fa-star"></i>Recommended</div><div class="tag preliminary">Preliminary
                <span>
                                      (Unknown/62 chp)
                                  </span></div></div>      <div class="text">
                About the Movement of the Earth is a historical manga, a rather underappreciated genre in the medium. But branding it merely as such takes away its allure – despite it being set in 15th Century Poland and exploring a historical finding that is commonplace knowledge now, the appeal of its central theme is both universal and timeless.<br />
<br />
The story does not take time in establishing that theme – an authority who is controlling the circulation of information, and heroes who are risking it all to bring the truth to light. The truth here being that Earth is, in fact, moving around the sun and is not
                  <span class="js-visible" style="margin-left: -4px;">...</span>
          <span class="js-hidden" style="display: none;">a stationary object at the centre of universe. The whole series maintains an anti-establishment tone about this discovery and the cost at which it came.  <br />
<br />
Story: 10/10<br />
<br />
It begins with Rafal, a prodigal student, encountering a former heretic who has been placed under the care of his foster father. From him, Rafal inherits the About the Movement of the Earth theory, something he first dismisses as heretical talk, but is soon fascinated with. But Rafal is not the definite protagonist of the manga. A single person didn’t bring the world to change, and it shows a lot of people contributing to the theory in their own capacities to complete it. One can say the Theory is in itself the protagonist here.<br />
<br />
The story is rebellious at heart, with its protagonists being both endearing and vulnerable, making us root for their success. It also helps that the story opens with the consequences of being caught by the Orthodox Catholic Church – the antagonist – while investigating this theory: labelled a heretic, brutally tortured, forced to reform, and burned alive if found guilty a second time (to cast off the devil who possesses a body and guide the man to propose such ideas). This is established from the get-go how bloody the history of such a revolutionary idea was.<br />
<br />
So, the stakes are really high from the start. But as a character in the story points out, why should anyone risk their entire life to secretly work on a “revolutionary” theory whose validity doesn’t affect anyone? True. Proving that earth moves would negate what Church had been teaching, but a farmer wouldn’t care about it. So why? <br />
<br />
The story follows different characters pursuing this theory, with each having a different answer to this – money, curiosity, rebellion, fame, contentment – but one similarity – the thirst for truth to come to light. The manga emphasises over and over that no matter the obstacles in the path, humanity will always seek out the truth. This makes for reckless heroes, but heroes nonetheless. <br />
<br />
Characters: 9/10<br />
<br />
The characters in the series are fantastically written. All have well developed convictions born from their struggles and surroundings. The author is never in a rush while doing a character background or backstories, and it shows how well-developed all of them are. Even the ones who do not appear for more than 4 chapters leave a lasting impression on the readers.<br />
<br />
But with a lack of singular protagonist, the character who stands out the most is the mercenary-turned-inquisitor Novak, the embodiment of the Church and the antagonist of the story. He is a man with a determination of a rock against those who indulge in heretical research. He is not a sadist, but is capable of gruesomely torturing people without giving them a chance to explain themselves. As a result, he is always looming over as a constant threat to the protagonists. But the reason he is such a good antagonist is the parallel development he gets throughout the story. We get to know who he is and why he has so strong convictions. And the reasons behind his convictions change over time, but it is done in a way that looks organic to his character. He also works as reader&#039;s viewpoint for all the faction politics going inside the Church, a subplot that was personally very intriguing to me.<br />
<br />
The series is unashamedly dialogue heavy. These usually involve the church’s teachings, maths, observational records, or philosophical debates. But the author uses a clever technique to not alienate the readers. For long conversations and debates, he makes one party an intellectual and the other naïve. A little boy who is a diligent student, an uneducated swordsman who fight rich folks’ duels for them, or a gypsy girl who don’t see any point in her identity as a Christian – all provide an easy self-insert for readers so that they don’t get estranged from the long dialogues.<br />
<br />
Art: 5/10<br />
<br />
One thing holding back this manga severely is the art. To put is easily, it is bland. It is not expressive and sometimes look like amateur doodles. I do not ask for art to be amazing in a series which is 80 per cent talking, but it is not even on par with those expectations.<br />
<br />
But credit where it is due, the panelling is very well done which helps a text heavy series like this immensely. It does not get tiresome to read, and panelling makes the emotional moments hit hard despite the mediocre art.<br />
<br />
Overall: 9/10<br />
<br />
In conclusion, go read this manga if you like historical fictions or people defying a medieval authority who censors the truth. <br />
<br />
Or if you just want to experience a good story. Especially if you want to experience a good story.</span>
